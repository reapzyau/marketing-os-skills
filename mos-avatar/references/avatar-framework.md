# TVML Customer Avatar Framework

> The Vibe Marketing Lab's proprietary customer avatar framework.

Your **Target Market Avatar** is the foundation for everything that follows -- offers, messaging, landing pages, ads. It captures who you're speaking to, what they want, what stops them, and the language they use. Do this well and your landing page becomes a digital appointment setter that converts.

Core principle: **Garbage in = garbage out.** AI accelerates you; it does not replace your judgment.

---

## Section 0: Avatar Overview

**What it is:** A structured 10-section profile of your dream customer that drives all downstream marketing decisions. The avatar is not a demographic sketch -- it's a deep psychographic map of your customer's inner world.

**Why it matters:** Marketing is inputs and outputs. The avatar is your #1 input. If you don't understand who you're talking to -- what keeps them up at night, what language they use, what they've tried and failed with -- your marketing won't land. The business that can articulate the customer's problem better than the customer can is automatically perceived as having the best solution.

**How to approach it:** Work through sections in order (1-10). Each section builds on the previous. Use internal resources first (questionnaires, sales calls, testimonials, NPS surveys), then validate with external research (industry reports, Reddit threads, competitor testimonials, articles). Read critically at every step -- AI is your assistant, not the authority.

**Quality checklist:**
- All 10 sections completed in order
- Each section grounded in real evidence (internal resources + external validation)
- Customer language used throughout (not marketing jargon)
- Any inferences clearly flagged
- Cross-section consistency verified (pain matches goals, Before State matches Pain Points)

---

## Section 1: Dream Client Name + Photo

**What it is:** A realistic first name and visual description for your avatar. This makes the avatar feel like a real person rather than an abstract "target market."

**Why it matters:** Naming your avatar forces specificity. When you write copy for "Sarah" instead of "women aged 25-40," your messaging becomes sharper and more personal.

**How to write it:**
- Give them a realistic first name (not aspirational, not stock-photo-sounding)
- Write a 1-sentence description of what they'd look like in a photo
- Third person voice
- Match the name and visual to your actual customer base

**Quality checklist:**
- Name feels realistic and grounded
- Photo description matches the demographics in Section 2
- Written in third person

---

## Section 2: Demographics

**What it is:** The factual "who" -- age, gender, location, education, occupation, income range, and other identifiers. This is the skeleton that all psychographic and emotional insights build on.

**Why it matters:** Demographics create the frame. Without them, your psychographic insights float without context. They also ground your avatar in verifiable data rather than assumptions.

**How to write it:**
- Cover: Gender, Age, Location, Education, Occupation, Income range
- Use factual bullets grounded in reference files
- Flag any demographics you inferred (not explicitly stated in source material)
- Validate against census data, industry reports, and market research

**Quality checklist:**
- All six demographic fields covered
- Each backed by evidence or clearly flagged as inferred
- Validated against external sources
- Specific enough to be useful (not "25-65, anywhere")

---

## Section 3: Crappy "Before State"

**What it is:** A snapshot of your dream customer's life before they work with you. The messy, frustrating, painful reality that makes them want to take action.

**Why it matters:** This section anchors your marketing in empathy. It shows you understand their world, builds trust ("they get me"), and sets up the contrast for your offer. The worse the "before," the more powerful the "after." This becomes raw material for your lead copy -- the Problem and Agitate parts of the P.A.S. formula (Problem, Agitate, Solve).

**How to write it:**

Formula: **Where they are stuck** + **How it feels** + **The consequences**

- Write a visceral 150-250 word flowing narrative in FIRST PERSON, present tense
- Write as the customer's inner monologue -- how they feel RIGHT NOW
- Use exact phrases from testimonials, sales calls, and audience research
- This is a narrative paragraph, NOT bullet points
- Cover their current struggles, the emotional weight, and what happens if nothing changes

**Example (Property Investment):**
"They've been researching for months, but never take action. Every weekend they promise themselves they'll buy, only to get overwhelmed and freeze. They're terrified of making the wrong choice, while property prices climb and inflation eats away at their savings. The result? Paralysis, stress, and the sinking feeling that everyone else is moving ahead while they fall further behind."

**Quality checklist:**
- 150-250 words, flowing narrative (not bullets)
- Written in first person, present tense
- Emotional, not just logical
- Feels like the customer would nod and say "That's me"
- Uses real customer language where available

## Section 4: Dream Outcome

**What it is:** The transformation your customer truly wants -- the vivid "after state" they imagine once they've solved their problem. This is not your product or service. It's the life they get because of your product or service. Ask: "What am I REALLY selling?"

**Why it matters:** People don't buy products. They buy transformations -- freedom, security, confidence, growth. A strong dream outcome answers the silent question: "So what? Why does this matter to me?" The clearer and more visceral you make it, the more irresistible your offer feels.

**How to write it:**
- Start with ONE clear sentence stating the transformation (choose one domain: Health, Wealth, or Relationships)
- Follow with a 100-150 word narrative in first person, present tense
- Describe life AFTER the transformation -- specific, emotional, vivid
- Follow the **Chain of Logic**: keep asking "so what?" at every step until you reach the deepest emotional payoff
- Include both practical wins (income, time, results) and emotional wins (freedom, peace of mind, legacy)

**Chain of Logic example:** First property --> portfolio --> passive income --> financial freedom --> don't have to work --> generational wealth --> family security. Each step answers "so what?" from the previous one.

**Quality checklist:**
- 1-sentence dream outcome + 100-150 word narrative
- Written in first person, present tense
- Includes both practical and emotional wins
- Chain of logic is complete (no "so what?" left unanswered)
- Feels inspiring and real enough to motivate action

## Section 5: Top 5 Pain Points

**What it is:** The biggest problems your dream customer feels in their own words as it relates to what you do. These are the frustrations, fears, and blocks that stop them from achieving their goals.

**Why it matters:** Pain points are the raw material for your copy. They fuel headlines, leads, bullets, and system explainers. They give you the "Problem" and "Agitate" parts of P.A.S. When customers read their exact struggle in your words, it feels like you've ripped a page from their diary. That's what we call "dog whistle language" -- phrasing so specific to their experience that it cuts through all the noise.

**How to write it:**
- 5 pain points, each with: **Bold headline (3-7 words)** + 1-2 sentence explainer
- Written in the customer's voice -- their words, their frustration
- Cover both emotional pains (fear, frustration) and practical pains (time, money, resources)
- Ground in testimonials, audience files, and real market research
- Use their exact language, not sanitized corporate versions

**Quality checklist:**
- 5 items in bold headline + explainer format
- Written in customer's voice (not jargon)
- Mix of emotional and practical pains
- Validated across internal + external sources
- Feels like "dog whistle language" -- they'd say "that's exactly my problem"

---

## Section 6: Top 5 Goals

**What it is:** The biggest outcomes your dream customer wants to achieve, in their own words. These answer the silent question: "So what? What's in it for me?" (WIIFM)

**Why it matters:** Pain points get attention, but goals create action. People move when they see what they want on the line. Goals become headlines, subheads, bullets, and case study framing. Even if competitors promise the same dream outcome, how you help them achieve it becomes your unique angle.

**How to write it:**
- 5 goals, each with: **Bold headline (3-7 words)** + 1-2 sentence explainer
- Include both spoken goals (what they'd tell a friend) and unspoken goals (what they secretly want)
- Every goal must pass the WIIFM test: does it clearly answer "What's in it for me?"
- Sometimes goals are the inverse of pain points -- that's fine, but they're not always

**Pro tip:** Don't avoid the obvious dream outcome just because competitors use it. Instead, differentiate with your proof, proprietary system, and unique mechanism. The dream outcome doesn't change -- your path to it does.

**Quality checklist:**
- 5 items in bold headline + explainer format
- Written in customer's language
- Mix of practical outcomes (build equity, save time) and emotional outcomes (security, confidence, freedom)
- Passes the WIIFM test
- Validated against real inputs

## Section 7: 5 Buyer Decision Questions

**What it is:** The critical questions running through your customer's mind before they'll take the next step with you. Your landing page is a digital salesperson -- these questions determine what it MUST answer.

**Why it matters:** These questions determine your messaging hierarchy -- what must be answered above the fold, in bullets, or throughout the copy. If you don't answer them, people assume the worst. Every bounce, no-show, or stalled deal is because you didn't answer these clearly enough. They almost always boil down to: Can I trust you? Do you know more than me? What can I afford? What should I choose? What if it goes wrong?

**How to write it:**
- 5 questions, each with: **Bold headline (3-7 words)** + 1-2 sentence explainer
- Written in the customer's voice -- their actual hesitations
- Order by priority -- address the biggest hesitation first
- Think: "What would a digital salesperson need to answer?"
- Consider whether the offer is "Discovery" (education-first) or "Ready Now" (specifics-first) -- this changes which questions dominate

**Quality checklist:**
- 5 items in bold headline + explainer format
- Written in customer's voice
- Ordered by priority (biggest hesitation first)
- Each answered directly somewhere on the page
- Builds confidence and clarity toward next step

---

## Section 8: What Have They Tried Before

**What it is:** The solutions your audience has already tried and failed with. This is not just a competitor list -- it's about showing prospects why you're not just better, but different.

**Why it matters:** If your offer sounds like what they've already tried and failed with, you've lost them. Your true competition is usually not Company A vs. Company B. It's: **Inertia** (doing nothing), **DIY** (trying to solve it themselves), and **The old/traditional way** (banks, agencies, generic programs). By naming what doesn't work, you position your unique mechanism as the new way forward.

**How to write it:**
- 5 items, each with: **Bold headline (3-7 words)** + 1-2 sentence explainer
- Matter-of-fact voice -- not their emotional voice, but objective
- Describe what they tried AND why it didn't work
- Use framing words like "traditional" or "typical" to group competitors without directly attacking
- End with positioning toward your unique approach as the reason this time is different

**Quality checklist:**
- 5 items in bold headline + explainer format
- Matter-of-fact tone (not emotional)
- Clear "why it failed" for each item
- Covers inertia, DIY, and traditional approaches where relevant
- Positions your mechanism as different (not just better)

## Section 9: Top 5 Roadblocks & Objections

**What it is:** The specific fears, doubts, and hesitations stopping your target market from saying yes. These are the "deal breakers" your digital salesperson must handle upfront.

**Why it matters:** Objections are buying signals -- they mean your audience is interested but needs reassurance. If you ignore objections, prospects stall or go elsewhere. If you name and overcome them directly, you remove friction and make it easy to say yes. Common patterns: time, money, trust, past failure, complexity.

**How to write it:**
- 5 items, each with: **Bold headline (3-7 words)** + 1-2 sentence explainer
- Written in the customer's voice -- their actual hesitations
- Pull from sales call transcripts ("Yeah, but..." moments), NPS surveys ("What hesitation did you have?"), and testimonials (concerns mentioned before purchasing)
- These become FAQ content, copy woven throughout the page, and sales materials

**Pro tip:** If trust is a major objection (and it usually is), your best tool is proof. Stack social proof, case studies, and credentials wherever the objection surfaces.

**Quality checklist:**
- 5 items in bold headline + explainer format
- Written in customer's voice
- Covers both practical barriers and mindset objections
- Each has a clear path to being overcome (proof, guarantee, or explanation)
- Validated against real sales conversations

## Section 10: Stage of Awareness

**What it is:** Where your prospect sits in their buying journey, mapped to Eugene Schwartz's framework from *Breakthrough Advertising*. Prospects don't wake up one day wanting what you have -- they move through stages. Your landing page and offer must meet them where they are.

**Why it matters:** If your message doesn't match their stage, you'll confuse or lose them. Correct awareness targeting means higher conversions and lower acquisition costs. Traffic temperature maps directly to awareness: Problem Aware = cold, Solution Aware = warm, Product Aware = hot, Most Aware = hottest.

**The four stages to map:**

- **Problem Aware:** "I know I have a problem, but I don't know the solutions yet." They're searching for answers. Great for consultation offers and educational content.
- **Solution Aware:** "I know solutions exist, but I don't know which is right for me." They're comparing categories of solutions. Great for comparison pages and consultation offers.
- **Product Aware:** "I know about your product, but I'm not sure it's right for me." They're comparing you to specific alternatives. Great for "Ready Now" offers with clear proof.
- **Most Aware:** "I know your product and I'm nearly ready to buy." They just need the right offer or a nudge. Great for remarketing, urgency, and special deals.

**How to write it:**
- Write each stage in FIRST PERSON as the customer's inner monologue
- 2-3 sentences per stage, specific to your market
- Don't decide which stage is "primary" -- present all four
- Use internal resources first, enrich with external data where gaps exist

**Quality checklist:**
- All 4 stages written in first person
- Each is specific to your market (not generic)
- 2-3 sentences per stage
- Reflects real customer language and journey
- Maps clearly to your traffic sources and offer type
